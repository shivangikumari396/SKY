Stroke

Overview

A stroke occurs when the blood supply to a part of the brain is interrupted or significantly reduced, depriving brain tissue of essential oxygen and nutrients. This leads to the rapid death of brain cells within minutes. Stroke is a critical medical emergency that requires immediate attention, as prompt treatment can minimize brain damage and other complications. Early intervention is key to reducing long-term impacts and improving outcomes. Encouragingly, the mortality rate from strokes has significantly declined in recent years, particularly in the United States. Advancements in medical treatments and therapies have not only saved lives but also helped in preventing disabilities associated with strokes, offering hope for recovery and better quality of life.

Symptoms

If you or someone you're with may be having a stroke, pay particular attention to the time the symptoms began. Some treatment options are most effective when given soon after a stroke begins. Signs and symptoms of stroke include:

  • Speech and Understanding Difficulties: Trouble speaking or comprehending others, which may manifest as confusion, slurred speech, or difficulty processing language.
  • Paralysis or Numbness: Sudden onset of numbness, weakness, or paralysis, typically affecting the face, arm, or leg, often on one side of the body.
  • Vision Problems: Sudden blurred, blackened, or double vision in one or both eyes.
  • Severe Headache: Intense and sudden headache that may be accompanied by vomiting, dizziness, or altered consciousness, potentially signaling a stroke.
  • Balance and Coordination Issues: Difficulty walking, loss of balance, sudden dizziness, or coordination problems.
