Kidney disease

Overview

Chronic kidney disease (CKD), also referred to as chronic kidney failure, describes the gradual and progressive decline in kidney function. The kidneys are vital organs responsible for filtering waste products and excess fluids from the bloodstream, which are then excreted through urine. In advanced stages of CKD, harmful levels of fluid, electrolytes, and waste substances accumulate in the body, leading to significant health complications. In its early stages, CKD is often asymptomatic, with noticeable symptoms usually appearing only after substantial loss of kidney function. Effective management focuses on slowing the progression of kidney damage by addressing the underlying causes, such as diabetes or hypertension. If untreated, CKD can progress to end-stage kidney failure, a life-threatening condition requiring either artificial filtration (dialysis) or a kidney transplant to sustain life.

Symptoms

Signs and symptoms of chronic kidney disease develop over time if kidney damage progresses slowly and may include:

  • Nausea and Vomiting: Digestive discomfort and episodes of vomiting.
  • Loss of appetite: A reduced desire or inability to eat.
  • Urinary changes: Variations in the frequency and quantity of urination.
  • Hypertension: Elevated blood pressure levels that are difficult to manage.
  • Cognitive decline: Reduced mental sharpness or clarity.
